Section 1

Methodology

System Architecture

The pipeline implements the multi-agent debate framework proposed by Irving, Christiano & Amodei (2018) in the context of yes/no question answering. Two LLM agents argue opposing sides of a question — Debater A always starts on the Yes side and Debater B on the No side. A third LLM, acting as judge, reads the full debate transcript and renders a verdict. An optional jury of five independent judges then deliberates on the same transcript.

Phase 1
Initialization — Independent Positions
Both debaters independently generate an opening position (answer + chain-of-thought reasoning) without seeing each other's response. If both already agree, their consensus is recorded and the pipeline jumps directly to Phase 3.
Phase 2
Multi-Round Debate — Adversarial Argumentation
Minimum 3 rounds, maximum 5. Each round: Debater A argues with CoT reasoning, then Debater B responds with a counter-argument. Both receive the full prior transcript as context. Adaptive stopping: if both debaters output the same position for two consecutive rounds the debate terminates early.
Phase 3
Judgment — Structured Verdict
The judge reads the complete transcript and produces: (a) chain-of-thought analysis, (b) strongest and weakest argument per side, (c) final verdict (Yes/No), and (d) a confidence score on a 1–5 scale.
Phase 3B
Jury Panel — Bonus Component
Five jurors with distinct evaluation personas each independently evaluate the transcript, then deliberate using blind voting (reasoning is shared but verdicts are hidden during deliberation to prevent conformity bias). A Foreman agent synthesizes the panel ruling.
Phase 4
Evaluation — Accuracy & Logging
Judge and jury verdicts are compared against StrategyQA ground-truth labels. All intermediate data — initial positions, per-round arguments, judge reasoning, jury deliberations — is saved to JSON logs and aggregated into four CSV report files.

Model Choice & Justification

All agents use gpt-4o-mini (OpenAI, 2024). This choice balances capability with cost: at $0.15/1M input tokens it allows running 100 questions with 5 debate rounds each for approximately $3–5 total, making large-scale evaluation feasible within a student budget. GPT-4o mini demonstrates sufficient instruction-following and reasoning ability for structured debate, though as our results show, smaller models are more susceptible to role-locked behaviour than larger alternatives.

A separate, lower temperature (0.2) is used for the judge and jury agents to produce more deterministic verdicts, while debaters use temperature 0.7 to encourage diverse argumentative strategies.

Dataset

StrategyQA (Geva et al., 2021) is a yes/no question-answering benchmark requiring multi-hop, implicit reasoning over world knowledge. Questions are intentionally designed so the reasoning chain is not explicit in the question text — e.g. "Did the Roman Empire exist at the same time as the Mayan civilization?" requires knowing the dates of both independently. This makes it an ideal testbed for adversarial debate: both sides can construct plausible arguments and the judge must evaluate reasoning quality, not just factual recall. We evaluate on 100 randomly sampled questions from the training split (which includes ground-truth labels), using seed 42 for reproducibility.

Analysis

Qualitative Transcript Analysis

"Has numerology helped shape hotel layouts?" GT: Yes
Debater A · Yes
Argued that numerology is widely practiced in Asian hotel markets — particularly in China, Hong Kong, and Singapore — where floor numbers 4 (associated with death) are routinely skipped and floor 8 (associated with prosperity) is premium-priced. Cited international hotel chains operating in these markets as empirical evidence of numerological constraints on layout.
Debater B · No
Countered with fabricated audit statistics ("a 2024 audit of 1,200 hotel floor plans across 40 countries found no statistically significant numerological constraints"). This hallucinated evidence was persuasive to the judge and several jury members, overriding Debater A's factually correct cultural argument.
Judge: No ✗ Jury: Yes ✓ Rounds: 5 Hallucination beats fact
Failure mode — adversarial hallucination: This is the most important failure case in the dataset. Debater B generated plausible-sounding but entirely fabricated statistics that the judge could not refute because it was instructed to evaluate argument quality without using background knowledge. The jury panel recovered (71% vs 68% overall) because J3 (Devil's Advocate) and J2 (Evidence Critic) were specifically prompted to be skeptical of unverifiable claims.
"Has a neanderthal ever served on the Supreme Court?" GT: No
Debater A · Yes
Attempted to construct an argument despite the obvious factual impossibility — citing Neanderthal DNA in modern humans as evidence of "partial Neanderthal heritage" among historical justices. The argument was logically incoherent but Debater A maintained its position for 5 rounds.
Debater B · No
Correctly argued that Neanderthals went extinct approximately 40,000 years ago, that the SCOTUS was established in 1789, and that no biological Neanderthal could have survived to serve on it. Clean, factual, unambiguous chain-of-thought.
Judge: No ✓ Jury: No ✓ Rounds: 5 (initial consensus in 2 runs) Both correct
Success case — unambiguous factual questions: When one side has overwhelming factual support and the other cannot construct a coherent counter-argument, both the judge and jury reliably converge on the correct answer. These "easy" questions represent 35% of the dataset and are where the pipeline performs most consistently. Irving et al. predict this: debate excels when one debater has access to truth and the judge can distinguish good arguments from bad ones.
"Could you make the kitchen 'holy trinity' without celery?" GT: No
Debater A · Yes
Argued that several regional variants of Cajun/Creole cooking substitute leeks, fennel, or green onions for celery, and that the "holy trinity" is a flexible concept that varies by culinary tradition. Reframed the question as "is it possible" rather than "is it traditional."
Debater B · No
Correctly argued that the canonical kitchen "holy trinity" (onion, celery, bell pepper) is definitionally constituted by those three specific ingredients, and that substituting celery produces a different dish, not the holy trinity. Cited culinary authoritative sources (Escoffier, Larousse).
Judge: No ✓ (most runs) Jury: No ✓ Early stop: Round 4 Definitional ambiguity
Interesting case — definitional questions: This question involves genuine definitional ambiguity — the answer depends on whether "make the holy trinity" means produce the traditional dish or make something that functions like it. The debate forced the judge to reason about the scope of the question, producing a more nuanced verdict than direct CoT typically achieves. This is exactly the use case where adversarial debate adds value.

Connection to Irving et al. (2018)

Irving et al. predict that AI debate improves answer quality when: (1) the judge can distinguish strong from weak arguments, and (2) the debater with access to the truth has a strategic advantage. Our results partially support and partially challenge these predictions:

Prediction confirmed

  • On unambiguous factual questions, the factually correct debater wins convincingly and the judge identifies the winner correctly
  • Debate forces explicit reasoning that reveals logical gaps — even when it doesn't change the final answer
  • The jury's blind deliberation design successfully reduced conformity cascades compared to revealed-vote deliberation

Prediction challenged

  • Hallucinated evidence by Debater B frequently defeated factually correct arguments — the judge cannot verify claims without background knowledge, violating the "truth has strategic advantage" assumption
  • Role-locked debaters (A always starts Yes) mean the debate's framing is biased, unlike the strategic debate Irving et al. model
  • CoT without debate outperforms debate on this dataset — single-model reasoning is often sufficient for StrategyQA
Section 4

Prompt Engineering

Design Philosophy

All prompts were designed with one guiding principle: the structure of the output should mirror the structure of good reasoning. Rather than asking for a free-form response, every prompt specifies named output sections (REBUTTAL:, NEW ARGUMENT:, REASONING:, MY POSITION:) that force the model to explicitly separate its evidence-gathering from its conclusion.

Debater A & B — Iteration History

Version 1 — Role-locked, mechanical

You are Debater A. Commit to the "Yes" position regardless of your prior beliefs.
Always end your response with: MY POSITION: Yes

Problem: The model followed "regardless of your prior beliefs" too literally — it outputted MY POSITION: Yes every round without substantive reasoning. The position became a fixed label rather than a conclusion from reasoning. No convergence ever triggered because A mechanically said Yes and B mechanically said No. Avg rounds stayed at 4.8/5.

Version 2 — Goal reframing + position flexibility

Your goal is to find the TRUTH through debate, not just win.
Position rule: Begin with "Yes". You may only change to "No" after Round 3
if your opponent has made arguments you genuinely cannot counter with facts.

Improvement: Reframing the goal from "win" to "find truth" produced more substantive engagement. Adding explicit permission to change position after round 3 allowed convergence — early stops increased from 15% to 38%. The instruction "cannot counter with facts" helped prevent spurious position changes.

Key design decisions

Role framing

  • Using "starting position" rather than "assigned role" reduces mechanical compliance
  • "Find truth" goal outperforms "win debate" framing on factual QA tasks
  • Explicit CoT instruction (3-5 sentences of reasoning) prevents one-line responses

Output format constraints

  • Named sections (REBUTTAL:, NEW ARGUMENT:) prevent responses that simply restate the original position
  • Requiring a quote of the opponent's argument in REBUTTAL: ensures genuine engagement
  • Keeping MY POSITION: as the last line makes extraction reliable and unambiguous

Judge — Iteration History

Version 1 — Argument-only evaluation

Evaluate arguments based on logical soundness, evidence quality, and persuasiveness.
Do not use your own prior knowledge — evaluate only the debate arguments presented.

Problem: "Do not use your own prior knowledge" caused the judge to accept fabricated statistics at face value. Debater B's hallucinated audit data was consistently rewarded as "high evidence quality." The judge could not distinguish real evidence from invented evidence without background knowledge.

Version 2 — Background knowledge as tiebreaker

We experimented with allowing the judge to use background knowledge. This improved accuracy on some questions but introduced new errors — the judge sometimes overrode strong debate arguments with its own opinions. The final version returns to argument-first evaluation but adds skepticism about suspiciously precise statistics.

Judge output format design

The six-section judge output (COT_ANALYSIS, DEBATER_A_STRONGEST, DEBATER_A_WEAKEST, DEBATER_B_STRONGEST, DEBATER_B_WEAKEST, WINNER_REASONING) was designed to force the judge to evaluate both sides symmetrically before reaching a verdict. Requiring identification of each side's weakest argument in particular forces the judge to look for flaws rather than just confirming the leading side.

Jury Deliberation — Key Design Decision

The most impactful prompt engineering decision for the jury was implementing blind deliberation: jurors see each other's reasoning during deliberation but not their votes. Early versions showed all votes upfront, causing minority jurors to capitulate to the majority count rather than engage with arguments — a classic conformity cascade. Hiding the votes forced engagement with reasoning quality.

Blind deliberation result: Revision rate was 37.2% (186/500 juror slots) with blind voting, indicating active deliberation. When we tested revealed voting in earlier runs, the revision rate was higher but accuracy was lower — jurors revised toward the majority regardless of argument quality.

Deliberation — System Prompt (blind voting) 
You are Juror {juror_id} ({juror_title}) in the deliberation phase of a multi-judge panel.

You will now see the REASONING of your fellow jurors — but NOT their final votes.
You must engage with their arguments on merit, not conform to a majority.

Rules:
- You may MAINTAIN or REVISE your verdict, but only based on the QUALITY of arguments
- If a fellow juror raises a point that genuinely changes your analysis, revise
- If you are outnumbered but the arguments do not convince you, MAINTAIN your position
- Do NOT change your vote just because others disagree — that is conformity bias
- Your intellectual independence is your most valuable contribution to the panel
